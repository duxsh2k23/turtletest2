Leatherback Sea Turtle


Leatherback sea turtles are the largest sea turtle species and can make a migratory journey of 7,000 miles in 10-12 months.


  • Kingdom:Animalia
  • Phylum:Chordata
  • Class: Reptilia
  • Order:Testudines
  • Family:Dermochelyidae
  • Adult Weight:Females 250 - 500 kgs (551 - 1102 lbs), Males up to 900 kgs (1985 lbs)
  • Adult Size:1.20 - 2.40 m ( 3.90 - 7.87 ft)
  • Appearance:Blunt head with visible pineal gland or ‘pink spot’, grayish black with white patches, leathery carapace with 7 ridges (5 dorsal and 2 on sides)
  • Diet:Jellyfish, salps, siphonophores, pyrosomes and other soft-bodied invertebrates

  • Natural Lifespan:100+ years
  • Age at Sexual Maturity:15 years
  • Average Clutch Size:60 - 90 eggs
  • Average Clutches per Season:3 - 10 clutches
  • Nest Incubation Period:70 days
  • Remigration Interval:2 - 5 years

  • Geographic Range:Leatherbacks live in tropical to sub-arctic zones, inhabiting waters off every continent except Antarctica. They are able to maintain warmer body temperatures in cold water due to thermoregulatory adaptations such as countercurrent heat exchange and insulating fat stores.
  • Marine Habitat Use:Leatherbacks are mostly found pelagic, but also found along continental shelves and in bays. They typically forage in the top 300 m of the water column and dive for 20-30 minutes at a time, but have been recorded diving as deep as 1,280 m (4,200 ft) and taking 86 minutes on a single breath.

The global population of leatherbacks has declined by approximately 40% over the past 50 years, with the Western Pacific population declining by more than 80% and the Easter Pacific population experiencing declines of more than 97%. Conservation efforts have focused on protecting leatherback nesting beaches, but for leatherback populations to recover, protections are needed at all life stages, including in foraging habitats and along migration routes. Wider stakeholder participation and coordination are needed to broaden conservation measures.

Green Sea Turtle


Did you know that green turtles got their name because they’re green on the inside? A diet primarily consisting of algae diet makes their internal fat stores green!


  • Kingdom:Animalia
  • Phylum:Chordata
  • Class: Reptilia
  • Order:Testudines
  • Family:Cheloniidae
  • Adult Weight:136 - 199 kgs (300 - 440 lbs)
  • Adult Size:0.9 - 1.2 m (3 - 4 ft)
  • Appearance:Rounded head, greenish-black coloration, smooth domed carapace with teardrop shape
  • Diet:Seagrasses, mangrove leaves and shoots, jellyfish and algae

  • Natural Lifespan:60 - 70 years
  • Age at Sexual Maturity:20 - 50 years
  • Average Clutch Size: 70 - 200 eggs
  • Average Clutches per Season:3 - 9 clutches
  • Nest Incubation Period:45 - 75 days
  • Remigration Interval:2 - 4 years

  • Geographic Range:Mostly in tropical zones, less often in subtropical zones, worldwide.
  • Marine Habitat Use:Coastal waters, bays, estuaries and inlets are home to abundant marine algae and seagrass. Young green turtles disperse into pelagic and neritic habitats along the continental shelf, where they feed on invertebrates before returning to coastal waters and adopting a mostly herbivorous diet as adults. East Pacific green turtles are known to bask onshore in protected areas when warmer waters are present.

Green turtles have been protected by treaties and laws to combat direct consumption of meat and eggs. However, illegal hunting of green sea turtles for personal consumption or sale in informal markets remains a challenge in many areas. Fishers may be reluctant to release other bycaught species of sea turtles when they fetch a high price locally. Demand for green sea turtle meat may increase in countries where Catholicism is practiced as a substitute for other forbidden meats on Fridays during Lent.

Hawksbill Sea Turtle


Often spotted near coral reefs, hawksbills are known for their exquisite shell pattern, featuring radiant streaks of orange and red. Targeted to fulfill demand for “tortoiseshell” trinkets, hawkbills are critically endangered worldwide


  • Kingdom:Animalia
  • Phylum:Chordata
  • Class: Reptilia
  • Order:Testudines
  • Family:Cheloniidae
  • Adult Weight: 45 - 68 kgs (100 - 150 lbs)
  • Adult Size:0.83 m (2.5 ft)
  • Appearance:Narrow head and beak, yellowish-brown coloration, carapace mottled with orange or red-tinted browns and overlapping scutes
  • Diet:Primarily sponges but also tunicates, mangrove shoots, algae and benthic invertebrates

  • Natural Lifespan:105 - 135 years
  • Age at Sexual Maturity:20 - 40 years
  • Average Clutch Size:140 - 200 eggs
  • Average Clutches per Season:3 - 5 clutches
  • Nest Incubation Period:60 days
  • Remigration Interval:2 - 3 years

  • Geographic Range:Tropical and subtropical waters worldwide.
  • Marine Habitat Use:Hawksbills are mainly found in coastal waters, including rocky zones, coral reefs and mangrove estuaries. They spend most of their lives in waters 20 m (65 ft) deep or less, but may also venture into pelagic zones. Juveniles and mature adults mainly occupy coastal zones, and the species is known for its coral reef dwelling abilities. Hawksbills feed on sponges, with the hook beak aiding their feeding.

Hawksbill conservation efforts are aided by the species listing in CITES, which outlawed international trade in Atlantic and Pacific hawksbills in 1977. Japan reduced imports from 37,700 turtles to 28,300 in the 1980s and 7,075 in the 1990s. Other nations involved in the trade of hawksbill shells followed suit, such as Cuba which decreased its official hawksbill fishery quota from 5,000 to 500 turtles. Illegal fishing continues to pose a threat to hawksbills globally, but in Cuba, many local people now claim hawksbills are worth more alive as a source of income generation from a growing tourism industry.

Olive Ridley Sea Turtle


Thousands of Olive Ridleys can show up at nesting beaches together during arribadas, nesting events timed around moon cycles. The word arribada means “arrival” in Spanish. The only other sea turtles known to nest in arribadas are Kemp’s Ridleys.


  • Kingdom:Animalia
  • Phylum:Chordata
  • Class: Reptilia
  • Order:Testudines
  • Family:Cheloniidae
  • Adult Weight:Up to 45 kgs (100 lbs)
  • Adult Size:0.6 - 0.76 m (2 - 2.5 ft)
  • Appearance:Triangular head with light cheeks, grayish-olive coloration, rounded carapace
  • Diet:Wide variety of crustaceans, mollusks, bryozoans and algae

  • Natural Lifespan:50 years
  • Age at Sexual Maturity:10 - 18 years
  • Average Clutch Size:100 - 110 eggs
  • Average Clutches per Season:1 - 3 clutches
  • Nest Incubation Period:50 - 60 days
  • Remigration Interval:1 - 8 years

  • Geographic Range:Tropical and subtropical waters worldwide.
  • Marine Habitat Use:Olive ridleys occupy a range of habitats to feed on benthic invertebrates and forage on snails and marine algae in shallower waters. They dive to 150 m (46 ft) to feed.

Olive ridleys are an abundant species with an omnivorous diet and ability to adapt to changing conditions. They can be solitary nesters or participate in arribadas, which can reduce the effects of predators targeting nesting females. They typically remain within 850 km (459 nmi) of their natal nesting beaches throughout their lifetime and benefit most from conservation measures as per the UN Law of the Sea. However, they are at risk of accidental capture due to their varied tastes and habitats. Substitution of J hooks with circle hooks can reduce rates of capture, but additional innovations are needed to reduce fisheries bycatch.

Loggerhead Sea Turtle


Loggerheads are the most widely distributed hard-shelled turtle. With disproportionately larger heads than other sea turtles, loggerheads use their powerful jaws to crack open the hard shells of molluscs and crustaceans


  • Kingdom:Animalia
  • Phylum:Chordata
  • Class: Reptilia
  • Order:Testudines
  • Family:Cheloniidae
  • Adult Weight:113 - 181 kgs (250 - 400 lbs)
  • Adult Size:1 - 1.1 m (3 - 3.5 ft)
  • Appearance:Broad head with yellow cheeks, red-brown, heart-shaped carapace
  • Diet:Mostly hard-shelled invertebrates, including crabs, mollusks and shrimp, but also jellyfish

  • Natural Lifespan:50 years
  • Age at Sexual Maturity:20 years
  • Average Clutch Size:100 - 125 eggs
  • Average Clutches per Season:2 - 6 clutches
  • Nest Incubation Period:60 days
  • Remigration Interval:2 - 4 years

  • Geographic Range:Mostly found in tropical and subtropical waters around the world, venturing into temperate waters to forage.
  • Marine Habitat Use:Loggerheads are pelagic creatures that spend most of their time in the top 5m of the water column feeding on jellyfish. They also dive below the thermocline for up to 30 minutes to feed on shellfish before returning to warmer surface waters to thermoregulate.

Loggerheads have benefited from protections, such as the SPAW Protocol to the Cartagena Convention, CITES Agreement, and Convention on Migratory Species (CMS). Logging bycatch and ship strikes are primary threats to loggerheads in marine environments. Turtle Excluder Devices on bottom trawlers are improving the odds for loggerheads in the East Pacific and North Atlantic Oceans. Additional monitoring at sea and targeted partnerships with fishing stakeholders in geographic areas where loggerheads face the highest risks could further improve conservation outcomes.
